Why I went low-level

After years of building for the web, I kept bumping into things I didn't understand. Pointers. Memory. What the CPU actually does. I decided to find out.

There's a moment every web developer eventually hits. You're debugging something — a memory leak, a weird performance cliff, a mysterious crash in production — and you realise you're guessing. Not reasoning. Guessing. I hit that wall one too many times and decided to go down.

The thing about abstractions

Abstractions are gifts. They let you build things without needing to understand everything underneath. But they have a cost: you can use them without understanding them. For a while, that's fine. Eventually, you start to feel it — a kind of helplessness when something goes wrong below the layer you know.

I wanted to stop feeling that. Not because low-level programming is inherently superior, but because I think you make better high-level decisions when you understand what's below you.

What I started with

I picked up K&R C. Then Computer Systems: A Programmer's Perspective. Then I started writing a small memory allocator from scratch — not to ship it, but to really understand what malloc does when I call it.

Writing malloc yourself is like disassembling a clock. Suddenly you see gears everywhere — in every abstraction you've ever used.

I'm not going to pretend it was smooth. C is unforgiving. Segfaults are informative in a brutal way. But the feedback loop is honest, which I've come to appreciate. It reminds me of climbing.

Where I am now

I'm still primarily a web developer. But when I write JavaScript now, I think about what the engine is doing. When I use a framework, I wonder about its allocations. These aren't intrusive thoughts — they're background context that makes me a better engineer.

Next up: Rust. I want to understand ownership at a deep level, not just well enough to silence the borrow checker.
